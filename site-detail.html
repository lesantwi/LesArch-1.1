About Le Pont du Gard

The Pont du Gard is an ancient Roman aqueduct bridge built in the 1st century AD to carry water over 50 km (31 mi) to the Roman colony of Nemausus (Nîmes). It crosses the Gardon River near the town of Vers-Pont-du-Gard in southern France.

Historical Context

Part of a nearly 50 km (31 mi) aqueduct system, the Pont du Gard was constructed by the Romans to supply the city of Nemausus (Nîmes) with water from the Eure spring near Uzès. Its construction is traditionally attributed to Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa, Augustus's son-in-law and right-hand man. The aqueduct system had a gradual decline in function after the 4th century AD but the bridge itself remained largely intact.

Engineering Marvel

Standing 48.8 m (160 ft) high, the Pont du Gard is the highest of all Roman aqueduct bridges and among the best preserved. It consists of three tiers of arches. The lower tier has six arches, the middle tier has eleven, and the upper tier has thirty-five smaller arches. The precision of its construction, with very little use of mortar, demonstrates the advanced engineering skills of the Romans.

Key Facts

  • **Construction Date:** Circa 19 AD
  • **Length:** 275 meters (902 ft) at its highest level
  • **Height:** 48.8 meters (160 ft)
  • **Material:** Local limestone (from the Estas quarry)
  • **UNESCO World Heritage Site:** Designated in 1985
