Le Pont du Gard

A masterpiece of Roman engineering in Southern France

Historical Significance

The Pont du Gard is an ancient Roman aqueduct bridge built in the 1st century AD to carry water over 50 km (31 mi) to the Roman colony of Nemausus (Nîmes). It is the highest of all Roman aqueduct bridges and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Its construction demonstrates exceptional Roman engineering skills and an understanding of hydraulics, allowing a precipitous flow of water with a very slight gradient.

Architecture & Construction

The aqueduct is constructed entirely without mortar, with its stones, some weighing up to 6 tons, precisely cut to fit together. It has three tiers of arches, with the top tier supporting the water channel. The bridge is a testament to the power and ingenuity of the Roman Empire, showcasing their ability to execute massive public works that served essential needs while also standing as enduring monuments.

Key Facts

  • **Location:** Vers-Pont-du-Gard, France
  • **Built:** 1st Century AD
  • **Height:** 48.8 meters (160 feet)
  • **Length:** 275 meters (902 feet) at its longest tier
  • **Purpose:** Roman aqueduct to supply Nîmes with water
  • **UNESCO World Heritage Site:** Since 1985
